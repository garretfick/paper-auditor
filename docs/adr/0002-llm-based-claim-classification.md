# LLM-Based Claim Classification

Identifying which sentences in a **Paper** are **Claims** that require a **Citation** is the foundation of the audit. We use an LLM to classify every sentence with a **Claim Type** (`Background`, `Method`, `Result`, `Discussion`, `Navigation`) rather than regex/heuristic rules. The reason a future reader might assume the opposite — "just grep for `\cite{}` and check coverage in the related-work section" — is that real papers don't respect those boundaries: background claims appear in introductions, methods, *and* discussion sections, and many sentences in the related-work section don't make claims at all (they're navigation, hedging, or definitions). Heuristic precision/recall on real papers is bad enough to make the audit untrustworthy. Trade-off accepted: every audit run incurs LLM cost proportional to paper length, and the classifier is the dominant source of noise in **Findings** (mitigated via the `Confidence` field).
