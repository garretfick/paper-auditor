# LLM-Based Claim Extraction

Identifying the **Claims** in a **Paper** is the foundation of the audit. We use an LLM to do this in one pass: it groups words into **Claims** and assigns each a **Claim Type** (`Background`, `Method`, `Result`, `Discussion`, `Navigation`). The reason a future reader might assume the opposite — "just split on sentence boundaries and grep for `[@cite]`" — is that real papers don't respect either boundary: a single sentence often carries multiple distinct Claims (compound sentences, and shared-context lists like "Several models — including BERT `[@a]`, GPT `[@b]`, and T5 `[@c]` — pretrain on large corpora"), one Claim often spans multiple sentences, and **Background**, **Method**, and **Discussion** material is freely interleaved at sub-sentence granularity. Heuristic precision/recall on either grouping or typing is bad enough to make the audit untrustworthy, and the two problems are coupled — you cannot classify a unit you have grouped wrong, so a separate "heuristic split + LLM classify" pipeline degrades to the worst of both. Trade-off accepted: every audit run incurs LLM cost proportional to paper length, and the Extractor is the dominant source of noise in **Findings**, now across both axes (mis-segmentation and mis-typing); the `Confidence` field on each Finding is the existing escape hatch, and per ADR-0004 we accept that prompt tuning is bounded by the local model's quirks.
